## Anti-Patterns Verdict

The page does not look like generic AI output. Its committed rose, gold, and teal palette, instrument typography, compass, and flat structural layouts feel authored. It avoids generic SaaS cards, glass effects, gradients, ghost shadows, and faux-terminal styling.

The AI-like risk is comprehensiveness: compass, modes, transitions, unknown taxonomy, guardrails, completion states, route, capability chain, loader map, and evidence taxonomy all try to explain the same system at different levels. Repeated large-heading-plus-explainer sections eventually feel generated by an impulse to include everything.

The deterministic detector returned nine advisory findings: seven hard-coded color occurrences representing five unique undocumented tonal values, one intentional full-pill radius, and one genuinely ordered six-step route misidentified as decorative numbering. The radius and numbering results are false positives; the undocumented tonal variants are valid design-system cleanup but do not cause the scroll problem.

No browser overlay was available. Existing 1440x5000 desktop and 500x6000 mobile Chrome renders provide the visual fallback. Neither reaches the footer; no visible overlap or horizontal overflow appears.

## Overall Impression

A first-time visitor can understand the broad promise from the hero and can choose among the four modes from the next section. After that, the page shifts from explainer to operating manual without acknowledging the change. The biggest opportunity is to separate the short decision journey from optional reference depth.

## What Is Working

- The hero clearly communicates the promise: discover the real task, protect unrelated work, make a small evidence-backed change, and report honestly.
- The mode chooser is the strongest comprehension device because it translates abstract mode names into recognizable task situations.
- Semantic color fields distinguish orientation, uncertainty, guardrails, and evidence while giving the page a recognizable identity.

## Priority Issues

### P1: The primary narrative is too long

Neither a 5000-pixel desktop capture nor a 6000-pixel mobile capture reaches the ending. Five substantial sections still follow the guardrails area.

Fix: keep Hero, Mode chooser, one worked example, a compact trust statement, and Installation in the primary narrative. Put deeper mechanics behind an explicit `Explore the operating model` disclosure or on a reference page.

Suggested command: `$impeccable distill`.

### P1: Too many conceptual models compete to be the explanation

Visitors must reconcile modes, unknowns, guardrails, completion states, route checkpoints, capabilities, progressive loading, and evidence boundaries.

Fix: establish one spine: `Choose a mode -> Work within guardrails -> Verify and report`. Make every retained concept subordinate to one of those three stages. Merge or move anything that does not fit immediately.

Suggested command: `$impeccable shape`.

### P1: Policy is explained before behavior is demonstrated

A visitor may repeat the vocabulary without knowing how the skill changes an actual coding request.

Fix: add one compact before-and-after example directly after the mode chooser: request, territory discovered, selected mode, smallest change, validation, and Field Report. Let that example replace several abstract explanations.

Suggested command: `$impeccable clarify`.

### P2: Mobile loses wayfinding where it needs it most

Internal navigation disappears while stacked content makes the page longer.

Fix: preserve a compact jump menu for `Mode`, `How it works`, `Evidence`, and `Install`, and repeat a small installation CTA after the short overview.

Suggested command: `$impeccable adapt`.

### P2: Verification boundaries are buried

The honest behavioral-evaluation limitation appears near the end, after earlier behavioral claims.

Fix: surface a one-line `What is verified today` disclosure near the first CTA and link to full evidence.

Suggested command: `$impeccable clarify`.

## Persona Red Flags

- **Jordan, first-timer:** the hero and mode statements land, but owning seam, supported cause, owned-diff review, and material unknown lack a concrete example. The number of frameworks can make a lightweight skill feel ceremonial.
- **Riley, stress tester:** the transparent limitation builds trust, but it appears too late to qualify earlier claims at the moment they are evaluated.
- **Casey, mobile visitor:** after 6000 pixels the page still has not reached route, delivery, loading, evidence, or installation. Hidden internal navigation provides no useful re-entry point after interruption.

## Minor Observations

- Desktop navigation is visually small relative to the hero.
- The mobile brand wraps awkwardly while the repository button dominates the header.
- Compass and transition labels become small on compact layouts.
- The long gold guardrails field makes that section feel even longer.
- The transition diagram loses much of its scan advantage when stacked vertically.
- Five undocumented tonal color values should become named design tokens during the next polish pass.

## Questions to Consider

- If visitors saw only the hero, mode chooser, one worked example, verification boundary, and installation CTA, would they understand the skill better?
- Is this primarily a landing page for deciding whether to install, or an operating manual for existing users?
- Which three concepts must a visitor remember tomorrow? Everything else should be subordinate, collapsed, or linked.
